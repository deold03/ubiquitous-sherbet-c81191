The most common mistake Chicago hosts make isn't in their listing photos or their guest communication — it's in their pricing. Most hosts either set a flat rate and forget it, or discount aggressively when they see empty nights. Neither approach maximizes revenue.

## The Flat-Rate Trap

A flat nightly rate feels simple. It's not — it's money left on the table during high-demand periods and a signal that something might be wrong during slow ones.

Chicago has enormous demand variation:
- **Lollapalooza weekend** in August can command 3–4x a normal August night
- **NFL Bears home games** at Soldier Field drive demand in the South Loop and Near South Side
- **Corporate travel seasons** (late September through mid-December) sustain weekday demand that leisure travel doesn't

If you're charging the same rate on a Lollapalooza Friday as a random February Tuesday, you're leaving significant money behind.

## How Dynamic Pricing Actually Works

Dynamic pricing tools — we use a combination of platforms — adjust your nightly rate in real time based on:

- Local demand signals (searches, booking pace in your area)
- Days out from arrival (rates typically rise as the check-in date approaches)
- Day of week patterns
- Competitor availability and pricing

But tools are only as good as the strategy behind them. We set floor rates that protect against giving away nights at rates that don't make sense, and ceiling rates that capture peak demand without pricing yourself out of the market entirely.

## Seasonality in Chicago

Chicago has distinct demand seasons:

**High season (June–August):** Summer tourism, outdoor events, rooftop season. Strong weekend demand, moderate weekday.

**Shoulder seasons (April–May, September–October):** Often the highest *RevPAR* seasons for well-positioned properties. Corporate travel is strong, rates can exceed summer weekend rates.

**Low season (November–March):** Demand drops, but doesn't disappear. This is where strategic pricing and minimum stay adjustments matter most.

## The Length-of-Stay Lever

Minimum stay settings are underused. A one-night minimum during a slow December fills gaps but may prevent longer bookings that are more profitable. Experimenting with two- and three-night minimums for peak weekends can significantly improve your average length of stay and reduce your cleaning cost per booking.
